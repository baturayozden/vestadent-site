What it is

A new root, not just a new tooth.

A dental implant is a small, biocompatible titanium post placed into the jawbone to act as an artificial tooth root. Over a healing period it fuses with the bone — a process called osseointegration — creating a stable foundation for a crown, bridge or full set of teeth.

Because the implant replaces the root as well as the visible tooth, it helps preserve the surrounding bone and lets you eat, speak and smile with confidence — without relying on or cutting down the neighbouring teeth, as a conventional bridge would.

Who it's for

You may be suitable if

  • ✓You have one or more missing teeth, or a tooth that can't be saved
  • ✓You'd prefer a fixed solution over a denture
  • ✓Your gums are healthy and you're committed to aftercare
  • ✓You want to protect the bone and shape of your face

How it works

Your treatment journey.

01

Consultation & 3D plan

A full assessment with 3D (CBCT) imaging to check your bone and plan the ideal implant position digitally.

02

Guided placement

The implant is placed under local anaesthetic, following the digital plan for accurate, controlled positioning.

03

Osseointegration

Over a healing period the implant fuses with the jawbone, becoming a stable, permanent root.

04

Crown & aftercare

Your custom metal-free crown is fitted, your bite checked, and a long-term care plan put in place.

How an implant fits together

The titanium post integrates with the jawbone to act as a new tooth root. An abutment connects it to a custom crown that sits naturally above the gum line — distributing the forces of chewing through the bone, much like a natural tooth.

Case types

From a single tooth to a full arch.

Single tooth

One implant and crown to replace a single missing tooth, without disturbing the healthy teeth on either side.

Multiple teeth

Several implants supporting individual crowns or an implant bridge to replace a few missing teeth in a row.

Full arch

A complete, fixed set of teeth on four or six implants — the long-term alternative to dentures.

FAQ

Common questions.

Are dental implants painful?

Treatment is carried out under local anaesthetic, so you should be comfortable during the procedure. Mild tenderness, swelling or bruising afterwards is normal and usually settles within a few days with simple pain relief. We'll give you clear aftercare advice.

How long does treatment take?

Implant treatment usually takes place over a few months, because the implant needs time to integrate with the bone before the final crown is fitted. Timescales vary between individuals depending on healing and whether any grafting is needed — we'll give you a personalised timeline at your planning appointment.

How long do dental implants last?

With good oral hygiene and regular check-ups, implants can last many years. Longevity varies from person to person depending on gum health, bone, habits and general health.

Can I have implants if I smoke?

You can, but smoking can slow healing and increase the risk of complications, which may affect the outcome. We'll talk this through honestly at your consultation; cutting down or stopping, especially around the time of treatment, helps healing.

What if I don't have enough bone?

Bone grafting or a sinus lift can often rebuild the area so an implant can be placed. We assess your bone with a 3D (CBCT) scan and let you know if this applies to you.

Am I too old for dental implants?

There's no upper age limit. What matters is your gum health, bone and general health — all of which we assess at your consultation.
